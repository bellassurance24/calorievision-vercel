Watermelon Calories: The Refreshing Truth About This Summer Favorite

Few foods capture the feeling of summer like watermelon. Its sweet taste, bright color, and hydrating qualities make it a favorite around the world. If you’re watching your health, you’ll be glad to know watermelon is low in calories and packed with nutrition. Here’s what you need to know about watermelon calories.

Impressively Low Calorie Content

A cup of diced watermelon has about 46 calories. This makes it one of the lowest-calorie fruits, so you can enjoy a generous serving without worrying about too many calories.

A classic watermelon wedge, like the kind you’d eat at a summer barbecue, has about 86 calories if it’s one inch thick. Even a big slice like this is still low in calories.

One hundred grams of watermelon provides only about 30 calories, among the lowest of any fruit per weight. This metric helps when comparing watermelon to other fruits or tracking intake precisely.

A whole mini watermelon, which weighs about 5 pounds and has about 3 pounds of edible fruit, contains about 410 calories. Most people wouldn’t eat a whole mini watermelon by themselves, but this shows you can eat a lot of watermelon for not many calories.

The calorie composMost of the calories in watermelon come from carbohydrates, with about 11 grams per cup. There’s very little protein or fat. Of those carbs, about 9 grams are natural sugars. So Low in Calories

Watermelon gets its name for a reason—it’s about 92% water. This high water content means it has very few calories for its size.

If you compare watermelon to other fruits, a medium banana has about 105 calories, an apple has 95, and a cup of grapes has 62. Watermelon’s 46 calories per cup are lower than those of almost all other fruits.

Because watermelon is mostly water, you can eat a big, filling portion for fewer calories than you’d get from a smaller serving of heavier foods. This makes it a great choice if you want to feel satisfied without eating too many calories.

Watermelon does have sugar, but not as much as you might think. Even though it tastes very sweet, it actually has less sugar per serving than some fruits that don’t taste as sweet. Its fresh, clean flavor makes it seem sweeter than it really is.

Hydration Benefits

Besides being low in calories, watermelon’s high water content helps keep you hydrated.

Eating watermelon contributes to daily fluid intake. While it shouldn't replace drinking water, watermelon can supplement hydration, especially during hot weather or physical activity.

Watermelon also has electrolytes like potassium, which help your body stay hydrated. The mix of water and electrolytes makes it a good snack after exercise.

For those who struggle to drink enough water, eating watermelon is a tasty way to help meet their hydration needs. Plus, it gives you nutrients that plain water doesn’t have. Studies suggest that watermelon juice may reduce muscle soreness after exercise, attributed to an amino acid called citrulline, which watermelon contains in notable amounts.

Nutritional Content Beyond Calories

Watermelon delivers more nutrition than you might expect from such a low-calorie fruit. antioxidant vitamin supports immune function, skin health, and iron absorption.

Vitamin A provides approximately 18% of daily needs through beta-carotene and other carotenoids. These compounds support eye health and immune function.

Lycopene is the antioxidant that gives watermelon its red color, and watermelon has a lot of it. In fact, it has more lycopene per serving than raw tomatoes, which are usually known for this nutrient. Research suggests lycopene may help with heart health and cancer prevention.

Citrulline is an amino acid in watermelon that turns into arginine in your body. This may help with blood flow and exercise performance. Watermelon is one of the best sources of citrulline you can eat.

A cup of watermelon gives you about 5% of your daily potassium needs, which helps regulate blood pressure and supports muscle function.

Even though watermelon is sweet, its effect on blood sugar is more complex than it seems.

Watermelon has a high glycemic index, around 72 to 80, which might worry people watching their blood sugar. But the glycemic index doesn’t tell the whole story.

Glycemic load, which accounts for actual carbohydrate content per serving, tells a different story. Watermelon's glycemic load is only about 5 per serving, considered low. The high water content means each serving contains relatively little total carbohydrate. For most people, even those with diabetes, eating a moderate amount of watermelon doesn’t cause big spikes in blood sugar. The important thing is to watch your portion size and see how your body reacts. Response.

If you have diabetes or are concerned about blood sugar, pay attention to how your body responds to watermelon. Many people can enjoy normal portions without problems, but everyone is different.
