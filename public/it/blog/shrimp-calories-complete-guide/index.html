Shrimp Calories: Your Complete Guide to This Lean Protein

Shrimp holds a special place among protein sources. It delivers substantial protein with few calories, making it a favorite for those watching their weight and seeking satisfying meals. Understanding shrimp's nutritional profile shows why this shellfish deserves regular rotation in your diet.

Impressively Low Calorie Content

Three ounces of plain cooked shrimp contain about 84 calories. This serving provides 18 grams of protein, creating one of the best protein-to-calorie ratios in any food.

A single large shrimp contains about 7 calories and 1.5 grams of protein. This makes counting calories simple when shrimp is your protein source.

One pound of cooked shrimp contains about 480 calories and 100 grams of protein. For context, a pound of shrimp is a substantial amount of food, likely more than most people eat at once.

One hundred grams of cooked shrimp provides about 99 calories, a useful metric for international readers or precise tracking.

Shrimp is mostly protein, with minimal fat and carbohydrates. A three-ounce serving has less than 1 gram of fat and no carbohydrates, making it essentially pure protein.

How Preparation Changes Everything

Plain shrimp is low-calorie, but restaurant and home preparations often change this dramatically.

Steamed or boiled shrimp keeps the base 84 calories per three-ounce serving. This method adds no calories and is the leanest way to enjoy shrimp.

Grilled shrimp with a light oil brushing adds about 20-40 calories per serving, depending on the oil used. The calorie addition is modest, and the flavor benefits are significant.

Restaurant shrimp dishes often have more calories than expected.

Shrimp cocktail remains one of the lowest-calorie restaurant options at approximately 100-120 calories for a typical appetizer portion with cocktail sauce.

Grilled shrimp as an entrée contains about 150-200 calories for a six-ounce portion, making it an excellent restaurant choice for calorie-conscious diners.

Coconut shrimp appetizers have 500-700 calories, with sweet dipping sauces adding more sugar and calories.

The Cholesterol Question

Shrimp contains more cholesterol than most seafood, about 166 mg per three-ounce serving. This high cholesterol content led to decades of advice to limit shrimp consumption.

Current understanding has evolved. Dietary cholesterol affects blood cholesterol less than previously thought for most people. Saturated and trans fat intake influences blood cholesterol more than cholesterol in food.

Shrimp contains minimal saturated fat, less than 0.5 grams per serving. This means shrimp's cholesterol comes without the saturated fat that drives blood cholesterol increases.

Major health organizations no longer recommend strict dietary cholesterol limits for healthy individuals. The American Heart Association considers shrimp acceptable as part of a heart-healthy diet.

For those with specific cholesterol concerns or familial hypercholesterolemia, individual guidance from healthcare providers remains appropriate.
